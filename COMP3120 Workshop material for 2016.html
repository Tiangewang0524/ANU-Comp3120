Introduction

Software development happens within a team and collaborative environment. Many times we do not learn what it means to work collaboratively and as part of a team: we are simply put in a team and are expected to be able to be productive. Team leadership and project management is another aspect of professional life where we rarely receive any instruction before the first time we are asked to take on those roles.

The purpose of this workshop is to introduce you to the concepts underpinning teamwork and the formation of high-performing teams. This week’s workshop is based on the readings and scenario included in your COMP3120 Workshop e-Book chapter entitled Workshop #1 – Teamwork and High Performing Teams.

Your tutor will bring printed copies of this worksheet to the workshop. You will need to ensure that you have access to the scenario and pre-workshop reading material either electronically or in hard copy.

For this workshop, you will work in your assignment teams. It will provide you with the opportunity to find out more about yourself and your teammates. You will also discover what roles best suit you and how you might going about ensuring that your team performs highly.

At the end of this workshop, you will understand what it means to work in a team, how personality affects what team roles you may be most suited to, how certain behaviours affect team operation and what is needed to develop a high-performing team.

Teamwork Scenario 

Excerpt from Appendix A: Coping with Hitchhikers and Couch Potatoes on Teams, It Takes Two To Tango: How ‘Good’ Students Enable Problematic Behaviour in Teams by Barbara Oakley, Ph.D., P.E. Assistant Professor of Engineering, Department of Electrical and Systems Engineering, Oakland University Rochester, Michigan 48309 (248) 370-2435 oakley@oakland.edu

In this handout, I will discuss at least two superficially similar types of problematic people. The first type is the ‘couch potato.’ If you think of yourself as tired and bored and really more interested in watching TV than working on your homework (everyone has had times like these), you begin to get a picture of the couch potato. A second, far more difficult type of person to deal with, is the ‘hitchhiker.’ Hitchhikers are called hitchhikers because they can get through engineering school by hitchhiking with various groups while contributing little to nothing themselves. Learning how to handle yourself with couch potato or hitchhiker types can save you a lot of grief and aid in more productive relationships. In the long run, changing your reactions to these people can prove beneficial not only for you but also for the person who is causing your problems. 

Jack, the Hitchhiker

To begin with, let’s imagine that you have been assigned to a combined homework and lab group this semester with three others: Mary, Henry, and Jack. Mary is okay—she’s not really that good at solving problems, but she tries as hard as she can, and she willingly does things such as taking the time to go in to see the professor or the grader to get questions answered when no one in the group can figure them out. Henry is a little more irritating. He’s a nice guy, but he just doesn’t put in the time or effort needed to do a very good job. He’ll sheepishly hand over partially worked homework problems and confess to spending the weekend riding his motorbike. Henry is so nice about it, though, that you can’t help but like him and cut him a little slack, although sometimes you get irritated and grumble to Mary. 

Jack, on the other hand, has been nothing but a problem. In fact, Jack is such a problem that we will focus only on him for a while because you will be able to use some of the same methods to deal with Jack (the hitchhiker) as you use with Henry (the couch potato). 

Here are a few of the things Jack has done:

  • When you tried to set up meeting times at the beginning of the semester, Jack always seemed to have something scheduled, so that he couldn’t meet with the rest of the group.
  • Jack infrequently turns in his homework. And when he does turn in homework, it is almost always wrong. In fact, he obviously spends less than two minutes on a problem—just enough to put some junk down on the paper that looks like work. 
  • Jack has never answered a phone message. When you confronted him about it, he denied getting any messages. You e-mailed him, but he never answered those, either. When you finally confronted him about the e-mails, he said he was “too busy to answer.” 
  • Jack doesn’t show up for meetings—even after everyone in the group has asked him to do so. (Last time he promised he would “be there for sure.” But he wasn’t there.) 
  • His writing skills are okay, but he just can’t seem to do anything right for the lab reports. He loses the drafts, or doesn’t reread his work, or leaves out the tables, or does something sloppy like write in the equations by pencil (“I don’t know how to use equation editor”). You’ve stopped assigning any work to him related to the lab report, because you don’t want to flub the strict deadlines your professor has imposed. 
  • Jack continually complains about his many problems, such as the fifty-hour work weeks at his job, along with the heavy workload from the three other classes he’s signed up for, the bad textbooks, and the terrible teachers. At first, you felt sorry for him—but recently it has begun to occur to you that Jack is using you. You’ve even begun to wonder if Jack has set up this inappropriate work/study schedule on purpose because he realizes he can get other people to do a lot of his work for him. 
  • Jack speaks loudly and self-confidently when you try to discuss his problems–he seems to think the problems are all the fault of the others in the group. He is so certain of himself that your fellow group members and you yourself almost wonder if it really is your fault. 
  • Your group finally was upset enough to discuss the situation with Professor Distracted. He, in turn, talked, along with the group, to Jack, who in very sincere and convincing fashion said that he just hadn’t really understood what everyone wanted him to do. Dr. Distracted stated that the problem must be that the group was not communicating effectively. He noticed that you, Mary, and Henry looked angry and agitated, while Jack simply looked bewildered, a little hurt, and not at all guilty. It was easy then for Dr. Distracted to conclude that this was a dysfunctional group, and that everyone was at fault—probably Jack least of all. 
  • The bottom line is: You and your teammates are left holding the bag—Jack is getting the same good grades as everyone else in the group without doing a lick of work. Oh yes— and he managed to make you all look bad while he was at it. 

Henry, the Couch Potato 

But we haven’t discussed Henry yet (remember Henry?). Although Henry stood up with the rest of the group to try to battle against Jack’s irrational behavior, he hasn’t really been pulling his full weight. Actually, Jack was so bad to work with that you didn’t even think about Henry’s occasional couch potato antics.

Introduction

The semester’s workshops are based on the Husky Air: Pilot Angels scenario. In parallel, we will use a metaphor or example of a very simple project with which we are all familiar – moving house – to introduce and illustrate some of the learnings.

The purpose of the first three workshops is to introduce you to and to give you some practice using an agile method for project planning called User Story Mapping which provides the team with a way to visualise the scope of the project and to show project priorities. During this first workshop we will be developing a “project backbone” and “walking skeleton”, which together provide a complete description of a project’s scope at the highest level.

During the initial phase of project planning the software to be developed, or “product” as it is often called, first needs to be described. In agile project management the product owner and senior members of the development team, for example, the project manager, the architect, and business analyst, will produce a high-level description of the scope of the project – what must be done, what will not or cannot be done. At this stage in agile project planning using the User Story Map approach, we aim to identify the smallest number of highest level functions sometimes called “Epics” or “Themes” that describe the full scope of the software to be developed.

The highest level of a User Story Map (USM) is called the “backbone” and comprises a number of vertebrae. The first level of decomposition of the USM is called the “walking skeleton” which provides a complete list of discrete (business) functions that describe what the software product must do to meet the business need. This is created by the product owner (and the stakeholders he or she represents) and the team who describe in a few, high-level functions what it is the product must do (its “functions”) to provide the required business outcomes (services and benefits).The product owner is responsible for the product backbone and walking skeleton: to ensure that one exists and contains suitable functional requirements.

The backbone and walking skeleton provide the highest level description of what the software will do – together they provide the “scope”. (Sometimes noting what is out of scope can be as useful to Users as noting what is in scope.) Subsequent further decomposition to lower levels adds “meat” to these “bones”. We sometimes find it easier to describe the walking skeleton first and group these functions to arrive at a backbone, and sometimes the other way round. Either way is fine, as is both simultaneously! Whatever works for you at the time.

All of the functions described by the backbone are too large for the team to estimate meaningfully and contain too little detail to describe precisely what will eventually be needed. These high-level functions will be “decomposed” into their component activities or functions on a “just-in-time” basis; that is they are decomposed only when greater detail is required. The team, often including the product owner, will break down these high-level functions into their component activities to add more detail, clearer definition and capture agreement and document better understanding of what they system must do. This is called “decomposition” and will eventually and repetitively throughout the life of the project, descend through many levels of increasing detail as the team approaches the “granules” of software that must be created.

Decomposition is an iterative process that continues throughout the life of the project and is the process through which we create what we will come to know as “User Stories” – the stories that tell what the users want and need. User stories provide a simple description of the desired function and are written from the point of view of the role of the person who needs the functionality. They can be written by the team, the product owner or by other stakeholders. The product owner assumes responsibility for the user stories but does not necessarily write all the stories.

User stories are a promise of a conversation around the story topic which, through the use of speech, aim to remove misunderstandings caused by writing. Stories are high-level and often abstract, but the conversation makes it tangible.

Once the team starts development work, they regularly need to revisit the story map and the stories it contains. This process is often called “grooming” and, among other things, involves decomposing larger stories into smaller ones that can be more easily understood and estimated.

Agile techniques acknowledge that not all detailed software functionality can be known at the beginning of a project. To attempt to do so is extremely wasteful of resources and, worse still, can lead to the delusion that the functions of the product are now fully understood, can be “signed off” by the stakeholders, and the product can now be coded and tested with these immutable requirements in mind!

In contrast, Agile uses a technique of decomposing to the next level of detail as and when needed, or “just in time” decomposition. This ensures that early, detailed functionality is described and delivered quickly to the users as working code, which immediately improves everyone’s understanding of what is needed next. As we then move through subsequent iterations of describe, build, deliver – the users will progressively accept increasing functionality with increased confidence – that is, the subsequent functional decompositions are much better informed.

This workshop will introduce you to some techniques for and provide you an opportunity to practice creating a backbone and walking skeleton through decomposition of very large user stories, which are known as Themes or Epics and form the backbone of a USM.

Your tutor will play the role of product owner. As you work through the activities in this workshop, David and Lynette, acting as product owner, will spend short amounts of time with each group to answer questions and explain what the product must do.

Case Study - Husky Air : Pilot Angels

Imagine you work for a small business, Soft Support, which provides general computer support - hardware purchase and installation; Commercial Off-the-shelf software supply and installation; plus general troubleshooting related to both hardware and software. Alongside the CEO and the receptionist and CEO's executive assistant, the company currently employs 10 staff to service their clients' needs. Soft Support currently has a client base of about 60 clients most of whom are small businesses like themselves. Recently the company has expanded into the market of supplying bespoke software development.

Soft Support has just won the tender to develop software for Husky Air to support their 'Pilot Angels' volunteer service.

Background

Husky Air opened for business in January 2004 when L.T. Scully and several other investors pooled their life savings and secured a rather large loan from a Canberra bank.

Located at Cooma airport, Husky Air is a fixed base operator (FBO) facility that offers a full range of services to the growing demands for business and private aviation. Currently, the company has 23 employees composed of pilots, mechanics, and office staff.

As an FBO, Husky Air provides:

  • Business jet, propjet, helicopter and propeller aircraft charter
  • Refuelling
  • Airframe, engine, propeller, and avionics maintenance
  • Aircraft rental
  • Flight instruction
  • Pilot supplies

Although FBOs at other airports offer similar services, Husky Air has been receiving increased attention throughout southern and western New South Wales for its charter service, maintenance, and flight instruction.

Pilot Angels

In addition, Husky Air coordinates a charitable service called Pilot Angels. Working with hospitals, health care agencies, and organ banks, Husky Air matches volunteer private pilots, willing to donate their time and aircraft, with needy people whose health care problems require them to travel to receive diagnostic or treatment services. In addition, Pilot Angels also will provide transportation for donor organs, supplies, and medical personnel. All flights are free of charge. Volunteer pilots use their own aircraft and pay all the costs.

Mission Statement:
Flight Angel is a charity that co-ordinates non-emergency flights for financially and medically needy people.

The pilots who volunteer for the Pilot Angel program need no medical training and offer no medical assistance. The planes do not carry any medical equipment and do not have to accommodate any stretchers. Patients, however, must be medically stable and able to enter and exit the aircraft with no or little assistance. The Pilot Angel passengers typically travel to or from a hospital or clinic for diagnosis, surgery, or some other treatment. Travel companions, such as a relative, friend or nurse, are common.

Currently, a pool of pilot volunteers is kept in a file folder. If a hospital or a person with a medical or financial hardship contacts Husky Air, the name of the traveller, the destination, dates/times, and the number of travel companions are requested. Because of limited weight restrictions in small aircraft, the weights of the passengers and their luggage are needed as well.

After the initial information is provided, Husky Air contacts the volunteer pilots to determine their availability. Although a volunteer pilot may be willing and available for a Pilot Angel flight, the plane may not have the range or weight-carrying requirements.     Introduction

    In a previous workshop (#2 -- Initial Project Planning, using the Husky Air scenario), we learned how to create a Product Backbone and a Product Walking Skeleton to describe the business scope of the software we intend to develop. The purpose of this workshop is to introduce you to and to give you some practice developing a product backlog and writing User Stories.

    The product owner is responsible for the product backlog: to ensure that one exists and contains suitable stories, but not necessarily to write all the stories. User stories provide a simple description of the desired feature and are written from the point of view of the person who needs the new functionality. They can be written by the team, the product owner or by other stakeholders. Remember, writing the story is less important than the discussions that surround its writing.

    User stories are written throughout the life of the project. During the initiation phase of a project, a “story-writing workshop” is often held. The purpose of this workshop is to create a product backlog that adequately describes the desired functionality of the project. Additional story-writing workshops will be held prior to the start of each release cycle to “groom” the backlog: to confirm the stories that already exist are appropriate, to remove those that are no longer appropriate for the goal of that release, and to add new stories based on changes since the previous version of the backlog was created.

    Although the product owner is responsible for the product backlog and ensuring that the stories it contains provide adequate coverage of the product, stakeholders, including the team and the product owner, write user stories collaboratively. This approach enables the involvement of domain specialists in the writing of the stories. User stories are written from the user’s point of view, but not a generic user: stories are written from the point of view of those with whom the system interacts and who may never actually use the system.

    Examples1 of user stories written for the design of a game:

    • “As a newbie player, I want to know who goes first so we can start the game.”
    • “As a competitive gamer, I want a way to leapfrog my opposing players.”

    Stories are usually written incrementally and include:

    • A brief description of the user need;
    • The conversations that take place during backlog grooming as part of release and iteration planning when the details of the stories are determined and confirmed; and
    • The acceptance criteria, or the tests, against which completion of the story is measured.

    This workshop will introduce you to some techniques for and provide you an opportunity to practice writing user stories.

We are going to do our estimation using a technique called "Planning Poker".

    According to Mike Cohn at (http://www.mountaingoatsoftware.com/agile/planning-poker/) Planning Poker is a consensus-based, agile estimating and planning technique.

    Top

    Planning Poker

    The whole team, including the product owner, take part. Each member of the development team (estimators) has a set of Planning Poker cards whose values are used to represent the number of story points. If a team doesn’t use story points for estimate, the values can represent ideal days, or other units in which the team estimates.

    The game begins with the product owner reading out a user story or describing a desired feature. The product owner then answers questions and provides additional information required by the estimators. Discussion of the story or feature continues until everyone feels comfortable that they fully understand what is required and the complexity of the story. When everyone is happy with their level of understanding, each estimator privately selects a card bearing the number of story points that represents their estimate of the size of the story under discussion. Estimators keep their cards hidden. Once all members have made their choice, all cards are revealed simultaneously.

    If all the estimators have chosen the same value this becomes the estimate for that story. Where complete agreement doesn't exist, further discussion takes place. Discussion centres on the estimates themselves with estimators sharing their reasons for having chosen a particular size. The product owner provides what further information the team requires to help them understand what is involved. A further round of estimating takes place with each estimator selecting a card (which may be different from round one). Again once everyone has made their selection, all cards are revealed simultaneously.

    The process of discussion and voting is repeated until consensus is achieved or until the estimators decide that estimating of this story should be held over until further required information is provided.

    The product owner’s role is to read out the stories, to answer questions and to provide sufficient detail so that the team feels confident to estimate the size of the story. While the product owner participates and has an important role in the estimating workshop, they do not take part in the estimation itself.

    When to play planning poker

    Planning poker can be played at multiple points throughout a project’s life. Initially, it is used when the product backlog is written to provide estimates used with scoping the project. As product user stories can be added to the product backlog through the project’s life, estimating is usually carried out at least once per release cycle. Some teams will use planning poker more frequently than this, once per iteration when beginning to plan for the next iteration.

    Top

    Benefits of playing planning poker to estimate

    It is claimed that using planning poker to estimate will improve the accuracy of a team’s estimation. One reason for this is that the complete team, composed of cross-functional experts, is involved. Additionally, the discussion that takes place during planning poker ensures better understanding by team members. Furthermore, being required to justify your decisions to your peers helps improve accuracy and helps compensate when information is missing. This is important on an agile project because the user stories being estimated are often intentionally vague.

    At the end of release planning, we have loosely allocated our coarsely grained stories to releases.     The iteration planning meeting, which usually lasts about an hour, produces the iteration plan. An iteration plan, which may be a spreadsheet or a set of note cards with a single task written on each card, provides a focused and detailed view of exactly what work is necessary to implement completely those stories that are included in the iteration. Importantly the detail relates only to the stories to be implemented in that iteration.

    The general sequence of activities for an iteration planning meeting is as follows:

    1. Where necessary, adjust any priorities
    2. Determine the iteration goal
    3. Discuss a story
    4. Disaggregate the story into its constituent tasks
    5. Estimate tasks -- Tasks are estimated in ideal-time.
    6. Add story to the iteration backlog.
    7. Repeat steps 3 to 6 until all stories to be included in the iteration have been discussed, broken into tasks and estimated.

    Discussion surrounding story decomposition and estimation is likely to include some design considerations. The task itself is not designed during this stage, all that is needed are guesses about what is needed to complete the feature.

    3. Select a user story

    Taking turns to act as the customer or product owner, each team member will read out the next highest priority story. The team asks questions of the product owner to ensure that they fully understand what is involved in the story.

    Assume that each story on your backlog is “ready”.

    Only select stories that are directly related to the iteration goal. Each story selected must add immediate value.

    Write the story on your worksheet.

    4. Split the story into tasks

    Decompose the selected story into the set of tasks necessary to complete the new functionality. During story decomposition, it is important that the team identify every task that will be needed to translate the story into a fully-functioning, and complete (i.e. meet the team’s definition of done) feature.

    Remember, if you are planning to deliver fully-functioning software that meets your definition of done you will need to include tasks related to testing (of different types, including unit and integration, UAT etc) and documenting the feature. Write each task on your worksheet under the story.

    5. Estimate the tasks

    The team should discuss each task and decide how many hours it will take to complete. When the team agrees, write that estimate on your worksheet alongside the appropriate task.

    Do this for each of the tasks you have identified for that story.

    6. Sum the estimates

    Sum the estimates for each of the tasks for the story you are discussing. Write the total number of ideal hours on your worksheet.

    7. Ask for team commitment

    Once all the tasks have been identified, estimated and the estimates summed, ask the team whether they can commit to delivering that feature (not tasks) during that iteration. Consider the story as a whole not a set of tasks.

    Remember you have estimated in ideal-time. When asking for commitment, you will need to allow for the other activities (such as attending meetings, writing emails, having lunch etc.) team members will need to complete.

    Assume that your team is only 70% productive. Assume the standard work day is 8 hours. If you are 70% productive, this means you can only schedule work for 5.6 hours per day.

    If the team can commit to the story, mark your story map with the iteration number.

    Repeat steps 3 to 7 until your iteration is complete

    Once you have completed planning the first MVP iteration, plan the second and third iteration until you have planned all the iterations for the MVP release.

    Key Learnings

    Students will know that:

    • when managing software development it is important to possess sufficient knowledge in sufficient detail before attempting to plan the day-to-day work of the team. That is, both the User Story and the Agile team must be completely "ready" before the User Story can be pulled into the iteration.
    • agile approaches require iteration estimation to be conducted using ideal-time rather that relative estimation values

    Students will understand that:

    • when planning an iteration task estimation needs to be conducted using ideal-time because the team must plan their actual work and story points do not have sufficient accuracy to enable this to be done with any 
    • an item of work is not included in the iteration until the team has "committed" to getting it "done" within the time constraints of the iteration

    Students will be able to do:

    • create an iteration plan that when completed will enable the team to deliver real, measurable value to the client
    • create a "committed" iteration plan that the team will be able to complete within a single iteration
